Introduction

Fundamental Definitions

  • A vector is a directed line segment with magnitude and direction

$$\vec{\textbf{v}} = \langle v_1, v_2, v_3,... v_n\rangle = \begin{pmatrix}v_1 \ v_2 \ v_3 \ .. \ v_n\end{pmatrix} = \sum_{i=0}^n v_i \hat{\textbf{e}_i}$$

Here, $n$ is the number of dimensions, $v_i$ is a scalar and $\hat{\textbf{e}_i}$ is a unit basis vector along an axis (a vector that moves exactly 1 unit distance from the origin to a point in an axis like $\hat{\textbf{i}}=\langle 1,0,0\rangle$ for $n=3$).

  • The magnitude of a vector denotes its length, which is equal to its Euclidean distance from the origin.

$$|\vec{\textbf{v}}| = \sqrt{\sum_{i=0}^n v_i^2}$$
- Unit Vector: Vector of length 1 in direction of $\vec{v}$

$$\hat{\textbf{u}} = \frac{\vec{\textbf{v}}}{|\vec{\vec{\textbf{v}}}|}$$
- Position Vector: Vector from origin $(0,0,0,..,0)$ to point $P(x_1,x_2,..,x_n)$

$$\vec{OP} = \begin{pmatrix}x_1 \ x_2 \ .. \ x_n\end{pmatrix}= \langle x_1, x_2,..,x_n \rangle$$

Core Operations

  • Addition and subtraction:

$$\vec{a} \pm \vec{b} = \begin{pmatrix}a_1\pm b_1 \ a_2\pm b_2 \ .. \ a_n\pm b_n\end{pmatrix}$$

  • Scalar multiplication: essentially scales magnitude by $|k|$ and preserves direction if $k > 0$, reverses if $k < 0$,

$$k\vec{v} = \begin{pmatrix}kv_1 \ kv_2 \ kv_3 \ .. \ kv_n\end{pmatrix}$$

  • Dot product measures the degree to which vectors are parallel (so, by definition, $\vec{a} \cdot \vec{b} = 0 \iff \vec{a} \perp \vec{b}$). Its computed as:

$$\vec{a} \cdot \vec{b} = \sum_{i=1}^n a_ib_i = |\vec{a}||\vec{b}|\cos\theta$$

  • The cross product is a binary operation on two vectors in three-dimensional space that produces a third vector perpendicular to both of them. This resulting vector is orthogonal to the input vectors and follows the right-hand rule for direction.
  • In 3D, the cross product can be computed using the determinant of a $3\times 3$ matrix, where the first row contains the basis vectors of the space, and the next two rows contain the components of the input vectors.

$$\vec{a} \times \vec{b} = \begin{vmatrix} \hat{i} & \hat{j} & \hat{k} \ a_x & a_y & a_z \ b_x & b_y & b_z \end{vmatrix} = (a_yb_z - a_zb_y)\hat{i} - (a_xb_z - a_zb_x)\hat{j} + (a_xb_y - a_yb_x)\hat{k}$$

Its magnitude can be calculated as $|\vec{a} \times \vec{b}| = |\vec{a}||\vec{b}|\sin\theta$ (it can be interpreted as the area of parallelogram spanned by $\vec{a}$ and $\vec{b}$). It is anticommutative: $\vec{a} \times \vec{b} = -(\vec{b} \times \vec{a})$

Vector Identities

  1. Commutativity:

$$\vec{a} + \vec{b} = \vec{b} + \vec{a}$$
$$\vec{a} \cdot \vec{b} = \vec{b} \cdot \vec{a}$$

  1. Anticommutativity of Cross Product:

$$\vec{a} \times \vec{b} = -\vec{b} \times \vec{a}$$

  1. Distributivity:

$$\vec{a} \cdot (\vec{b} + \vec{c}) = \vec{a} \cdot \vec{b} + \vec{a} \cdot \vec{c}$$
$$\vec{a} \times (\vec{b} + \vec{c}) = \vec{a} \times \vec{b} + \vec{a} \times \vec{c}$$

  1. Scalar Multiplication:
    $$k(\vec{a} \cdot \vec{b}) = (k\vec{a}) \cdot \vec{b} = \vec{a} \cdot (k\vec{b})$$
    $$k(\vec{a} \times \vec{b}) = (k\vec{a}) \times \vec{b} = \vec{a} \times (k\vec{b})$$

$$\vec{a} \cdot (\vec{b} \times \vec{c}) = \begin{vmatrix} a_x & a_y & a_z \ b_x & b_y & b_z \ c_x & c_y & c_z \end{vmatrix}$$

Key Properties:
- Cyclic symmetry:

$$\vec{a}\cdot(\vec{b}\times\vec{c}) = \vec{b}\cdot(\vec{c}\times\vec{a}) = \vec{c}\cdot(\vec{a}\times\vec{b})$$
- Antisymmetry:

$$\vec{a}\cdot(\vec{b}\times\vec{c}) = -\vec{a}\cdot(\vec{c}\times\vec{b})$$
- Vanishes if vectors are coplanar

Lagrange's Identity:

$$\vec{a} \times (\vec{b} \times \vec{c}) = (\vec{a} \cdot \vec{c})\vec{b} - (\vec{a} \cdot \vec{b})\vec{c}$$
- Non-associativity:

$$\vec{a} \times (\vec{b} \times \vec{c}) \neq (\vec{a} \times \vec{b}) \times \vec{c}$$

Geometric Entities

Lines

Parametric equation through $P_0(x_0,y_0,z_0)$ with direction $\vec{d} = \langle a,b,c \rangle$:

$$\vec{r}(t) = \vec{r_0} + t\vec{d}
\Rightarrow \begin{cases} x = x_0 + at \ y = y_0 + bt \ z = z_0 + ct \end{cases}, \quad t \in \mathbb{R}$$

Planes

  • Defined by a point $P_0(x_0,y_0,z_0)$ and normal vector $\vec{n} = \langle a,b,c \rangle$. Unlike a line, its defined by two direction vectors, and hence spans a two dimensional region in space. In vector form:

$$\vec{r}(t,\mu) = \begin{pmatrix}x_o \ y_o \ z_o\end{pmatrix} + t \begin{pmatrix}d_x \ d_y \ d_z\end{pmatrix}+ \mu \begin{pmatrix}e_x \ e_y \ e_z\end{pmatrix}$$

  • Its standard form, for normal vector coefficients $\hat{n}=\langle a, b, c\rangle$ is given by:

$$a(x-x_0) + b(y-y_0) + c(z-z_0) = 0$$

  • And its general form is $ax + by + cz + d = 0$

Distance Formulae

  1. Point to Plane:

$$\text{Distance} = \frac{|ax_0 + by_0 + cz_0 + d|}{\sqrt{a^2 + b^2 + c^2}}$$

  1. Point to Line:

$$\text{Distance} = \frac{| (\vec{r} - \vec{r_0}) \times \vec{d} |}{|\vec{d}|}$$

  1. Skew Lines:

$$\text{Distance} = \frac{| (\vec{r_2} - \vec{r_1}) \cdot (\vec{d_1} \times \vec{d_2}) |}{|\vec{d_1} \times \vec{d_2}|}$$

  1. Two parallel planes: Given planes: $P_1: ax + by + cz + d_1 = 0$, $P_2: ax + by + cz + d_2 = 0$ (Same normal $\vec{n} = \langle a,b,c \rangle$)

$$D=\frac{|d_1-d_2|}{\sqrt{a^2+b^2+c^2}}$$

  1. Line to plane (parallel): Plane: $ax + by + cz + d = 0$, line: $\vec{r}(t) = \langle x_0,y_0,z_0 \rangle + t\langle v_x,v_y,v_z \rangle$ with $\vec{n} \perp \vec{v}$ (i.e., $av_x + bv_y + cv_z = 0$).

$$D=\frac{|ax_o+by_o+cz_o+d|}{\sqrt{a^2+b^2+c^2}}$$

Vector Functions

What is a Vector Function?

  • A vector function is a parametrically defined function where the terminal points of vectors trace a curve instead of a surface in 3-dimensional space.
  • The fact that $t$ has a certain domain gives $r(t)$ an orientation.

Sketching a Vector Function

  • Identify $x, y, z$.
  • Use one or more components to get a curve or surface (eliminate $t$).
  • For just two components, sketch on a plane.
  • For three components, it's a curve traveling along a surface.
  • Use values of $t$ to find points and the orientation.

Derivative of a Vector Function

  • The derivative of a vector function describes the direction vector of the tangent vector to the curve at some value of $t$.
  • The derivative of a function is defined as:

$$f'(x) = \lim_{\Delta x \to 0} \frac{f(x+\Delta x) - f(x)}{\Delta x}$$

  • Similarly, the derivative of a vector function is defined for some parameter $t$ as:

$$ r'(t) = \lim_{\Delta t \to 0} \frac{r(t+\Delta t) - r(t)}{\Delta t} = \lim_{\Delta t \to 0} \frac{f(t+\Delta t) \hat{i} + g(t+\Delta t) \hat{j} + h(t+\Delta t) \hat{k} - (f(t) \hat{i} + g(t) \hat{j} + h(t) \hat{k})}{\Delta t} $$

$$ = \lim_{\Delta t \to 0} \frac{f(t+\Delta t)-f(t)}{\Delta t} \hat{i} + \frac{g(t+\Delta t)-g(t)}{\Delta t} \hat{j} + \frac{h(t+\Delta t)-h(t)}{\Delta t} \hat{k} = f'(t)\hat{i} + g'(t) \hat{j} + h'(t) \hat{k} $$

  • Example: The derivative of $r(t) = t \hat{i} + t^2 \hat{j} + t^3 \hat{k}$ is $r'(t) = \hat{i} + 2t\hat{j} + 3t^2 \hat{k}$.

The Arc Length of a Curve

  • For some vector function $r(t)$ with a derivative $r'(t)$, the so-called 'magnitude' of $r'(t)$ is expressed as:

$$||r'(t)|| = \sqrt{(f'(t))^2 + (g'(t))^2 + (h'(t))^2}$$

  • The arc length of the curve generated by the vector function, for some interval $t \in [a,b]$, is given as:

$$L=\int_a^b \sqrt{(f'(t))^2 + (g'(t))^2 + (h'(t))^2} \, dt = \int_a^b ||r'(t)|| \, dt$$

Arc Length Reparametrization

Why? - We can "walk through the curve" instead of using a vector to point at each individual point.

How? - Find the arc length function ($S(t)$, not $L$):

$$S(t)=\int_a^t ||r(u)|| \, du$$

  • $a$ is where $t$ begins.

Example

For the vector function:

$$r(t) = e^t \cos{t} \hat{i} + e^t \sin{t} \hat{j} + e^t \hat{k} \text{ for } t \geq 0$$
1. Compute $||r'(t)||$:

$$||r'(t)|| = \sqrt{3}e^t$$

  1. Compute the arc length function $S(t)$:

$$S(t) = \int_0^t \sqrt{3} e^u \, du = \sqrt{3}(e^t-1)$$

  1. Turn $S(t)$ into the parameter $S$:
    $$S=\sqrt{3}(e^t-1)$$
    Solve for $t$:

$$e^t = \frac{\sqrt{3}}{3} S + 1$$
$$t=\ln{\left(\frac{\sqrt{3}}{3} S +1\right)}$$

  1. Substitute back into $r(t)$:
    $$ r(s) = \left(\frac{\sqrt{3}}{3}S+1\right)\cos{\left(\ln{\left(\frac{\sqrt{3}}{3} S +1\right)}\right)}\hat{i} + \left(\frac{\sqrt{3}}{3}S+1\right)\sin{\left(\ln{\left(\frac{\sqrt{3}}{3} S +1\right)}\right)}\hat{j}+\left(\frac{\sqrt{3}}{3}S+1\right)\hat{k} $$

Vector Fields

Divergence

  • A measurement of how much fluid/flow enters the neighborhood around $P$ compared to how much leaves.
  • If more fluid enters than leaves, then the divergence will be negative at $P$.
  • If the same amount of fluid enters and leaves, then the divergence will be $0$ at $P$.
  • For

$$ F(x, y, z) = P\hat{i} + Q\hat{j} + R\hat{k} $$

$$ Div(F) = \nabla \cdot F = \frac{\partial P}{\partial x} + \frac{\partial Q}{\partial y} + \frac{\partial R}{\partial z} $$

  • It is a scalar quantity.

Curl

  • A measurement of the rotation of the vector field in the neighborhood around $P$.
  • If curl is $+$ at a point, then the flow would rotate counterclockwise.
  • And if $-$, then clockwise.
  • If $0$, then there is no rotation at $P$.

$$ Curl(F) = \nabla \times F $$

  • It is a vector.

Work Done to Move along a Vector Field

Non-Conservative Vector Fields

  • For a non-conservative vector field $F(x, y, z) = F(x(t), y(t), z(t))$ along a path $r(t)$, the work done for a particle to move along the given path $C$ is given by:

$$ w = \int_C F \cdot dr = \int_{t=a}^{t=b} F(r(t)) \cdot r'(t) \cdot dt $$

  • These integrals are path dependent.

Conservative Vector Fields

  • $F(x, y)$ is conservative iff $F(x, y) = \nabla f(x, y)$ for some $f(x, y)$ that is called a potential function.
  • This means a line integral within a conservative vector field can be expressed by:

$$ \int_C F \cdot dr = \int_C \nabla f \cdot dr = f(b) - f(a) $$

Showing $F$ is Conservative

  • For a conservative $F(x, y) = P\hat{i} + Q\hat{j}$:

$$ \frac{\partial P}{\partial y} = \frac{\partial Q}{\partial x} $$

  • Or for $F(x, y, z) = P\hat{i} + Q\hat{j} + R\hat{k}$:

$$ Curl(F) = 0 $$
