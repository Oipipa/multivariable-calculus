Multivariable Differenciation

The Idea

  • Multivariable functions take $n$ independent variables to return the value of one dependent variable.
  • While a function $f(x)$ may be a line or, more generally, a curve in 2-D, a function $f(x, y)$ will extend to 3-D as a surface.
  • The derivative of this function at some point wouldn't simply be the gradient of the tangent line to the surface, as it'd be too ambiguous due to the number of tangent lines that can be drawn on the surface, all with varying directions.
  • To find the gradient of the tangent line to a surface at a point, we have to give it a direction.
  • This direction can, in the most simple cases, be along $\hat{i}$ or $\hat{j}$ in 3-D, or it can extend along any unit vector in the plane of the independent variables as a general directional derivative.

Directional Derivatives Along $\hat{i}$ and $\hat{j}$

  • When calculating the gradient along the $x$ direction (along $\hat{i}$), the tangent line is constrained to a plane parallel to the $xz$ plane.
  • Since this only contains the $x$ axis, $y$ must be treated as constant.
  • Similarly, when calculating the gradient along the $y$ direction, $x$ is treated as constant.
  • This idea of treating a variable as a constant ensures the tangent line is in the direction of the other variable and is called a partial derivative.
  • Notation for a partial derivative with respect to $x$:

$$ \frac{\partial f}{\partial x} \text{ or } f_x $$

Directional Derivatives Along $\hat{u}$

  • Extending the idea of calculating the derivative along a single independent variable, directional derivatives can be calculated along any direction $\hat{u} = u_1 \hat{i} + u_2 \hat{j}$, where $\hat{u}$ is a unit vector.
  • For a function $f(x, y)$ and vector $\hat{u}$, the directional derivative is denoted as $D_{\hat{u}} f(x, y)$ and is calculated as:

$$ D_{\hat{u}} f(x, y) = f_x u_1 + f_y u_2 $$

  • This expression can be rewritten as a dot product:

$$ D_{\hat{u}} f(x, y) = (f_x \hat{i} + f_y \hat{j}) \cdot (u_1 \hat{i} + u_2 \hat{j}) $$

  • The vector $f_x \hat{i} + f_y \hat{j}$ is called the gradient vector and is denoted as $\nabla f$. Thus:

$$ D_{\hat{u}} f(x, y) = \nabla f \cdot \hat{u} $$

Tangent Lines and Planes

  • $\nabla f$ is the normal to the level curve of $f(x, y) = c$.
  • $\nabla f$ is the normal to the level surface of $f(x, y, z) = d$.
  • Tangent lines and planes both require the normal vector.

Example: Determining Tangent and Normal Lines (2-D)

  • Consider $x^2 - y^2 = 16$. To find the tangent and normal lines, treat this as a level curve of some surface in $\mathbb{R}^3$: $f(x, y) = x^2 - y^2$.
  • The gradient vector $\nabla f(x, y) = 2x \hat{i} - 2y \hat{j}$ is the normal to any level curve $x^2 - y^2 = c$.
  • At point $P(5, 3)$, $\nabla f(5, 3)$ is:

$$ \nabla f(5, 3) = 10 \hat{i} - 6 \hat{j} $$

  • The gradient of the normal line is:

$$ m_N = \frac{-6}{10} = \frac{-3}{5} $$

  • Equation of the normal line: $$ y - 3 = -\frac{3}{5}(x - 5) \Rightarrow y = -\frac{3}{5}x + 6 $$

  • The tangent line is perpendicular to the normal line, so its gradient is $\frac{5}{3}$. The equation of the tangent line is: $$ y - 3 = \frac{5}{3}(x - 5) \Rightarrow y = \frac{5}{3}x - \frac{16}{3} $$

Example: Determining Tangent Planes (3-D)

  • Consider $-x^2 + y^2 - z^2 = 4$ at point $P(1, 3, 2)$. Treat this as a level surface in $\mathbb{R}^4$: $f(x, y, z) = -x^2 + y^2 - z^2$.
  • $\nabla f(1, 3, 2)$ gives the normal vector:

$$ \nabla f = -2 \hat{i} + 6 \hat{j} - 4 \hat{k} $$

  • The tangent plane equation is given by: $$ a(x - x_0) + b(y - y_0) + c(z - z_0) = 0 $$

  • Substituting values: $$ -2(x - 1) + 6(y - 3) - 4(z - 2) = 0 $$

Simplifies to:

$$ -2x + 6y - 4z = 8 $$

1 Variable Functions 2 Variable Functions
Relative maximum is the peak of a curve Relative maximum is the peak of a surface
Given at critical points where $f'(x) = 0$ or where $f'(x)$ is undefined in the domain of the function Given at critical points where $f_x = f_y = 0$ or where $f_x$ or $f_y$ are undefined in the domain of the function.

$2^\text{nd}$ Derivative Test in 3-D

  • When $f_x$ and $f_y$ go from $+ \to -$, it is a relative maximum.
  • When $f_x$ and $f_y$ go from $- \to +$, it is a relative minimum.
  • When $f_x$ goes from $+ \to -$ but $f_y$ goes from $- \to +$ (or vice versa), we have a saddle.

The second derivative test for two-variable functions is given as:

$$ D(x, y) = f_{xx}(x, y) \cdot f_{yy}(x, y) - (f_{xy})^2 $$

  1. When $D(a, b) > 0$, $f_{xx}(a, b) > 0$, $(a, b)$ gives a relative minimum.
  2. When $D(a, b) > 0$, $f_{xx}(a, b) < 0$, $(a, b)$ gives a relative maximum.
  3. When $D(a, b) < 0$, $(a, b)$ gives a saddle.
  4. When $D(a, b) = 0$, results are inconclusive.

Constrained Optimization

The Idea

  • $f(x, y) = z$ is a surface and $g(x, y) = c$ is a level curve to a surface $g(x, y) = z$.
  • The intersection of the level curve of $g(x, y)$ and $f(x, y)$ gives a constrained maxima and minima.
  • The normals of the level curves are scalar multiples of each other. That is to say:

$$ \nabla f(x, y) = \lambda \nabla g(x, y) $$

  • This $\lambda$ is called a Lagrange multiplier. The solution to this will give a maximum and minimum.

Example Problem

$f(x, y, z) = x + 2y - 2z$ with a constraint $x^2 + 2y^2 + 4z^2 = 1$.

Treat $x^2 + 2y^2 + 4z^2 = 1$ as a level surface to $g(x, y, z) = w$.

$$ \nabla f(x, y, z) = \lambda \nabla g(x, y, z) $$

$$ \hat{i} + 2\hat{j} - 2\hat{k} = \lambda (2x\hat{i} + 4y\hat{j} + 8z\hat{k}) $$

$$ \begin{matrix} 2x\lambda = 1 \Rightarrow \lambda = \frac{1}{2x} \\ 4y\lambda = 2 \Rightarrow \lambda = \frac{1}{2y} \\ 8z\lambda = -2 \Rightarrow \lambda = -\frac{1}{4z} \end{matrix} $$

$$ \left(\frac{1}{2\lambda}\right)^2 + 2\left(\frac{1}{2\lambda}\right)^2 + 4\left(-\frac{1}{4\lambda}\right)^2 = 1 $$

$$ \lambda = \pm 1 $$

For $\lambda = 1$, we get $(\frac{1}{2}, \frac{1}{2}, -\frac{1}{4})$, and for $\lambda = -1$, $(-\frac{1}{2}, -\frac{1}{2}, \frac{1}{4})$.
